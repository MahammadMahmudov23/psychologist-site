Self-help

Quieting anxious thoughts

By [Name], Psychologist · 3 min read

Anxiety isn’t a flaw or a weakness — it’s your mind trying, a little too hard, to keep you safe. You can’t always switch it off, but you can learn to turn the volume down. Here are three small practices that help in the moment.

1. Name it to tame it

When anxiety rises, quietly label what’s happening: “this is anxiety, and it will pass.” Naming an emotion gently moves your brain out of alarm mode and into a steadier, more reflective place.

2. Come back to your senses

Look around and notice five things you can see, four you can hear, three you can touch. This simple grounding exercise pulls you out of the spiral of thoughts and back into the present, where you’re actually safe.

  • Slow your out-breath — make it longer than your in-breath.
  • Unclench your jaw and drop your shoulders.
  • Place a hand on your chest; feel it rise and fall.
You don’t have to do this perfectly. Even a little softening is enough — and it gets easier with practice.

3. Be on your own side

Notice how you talk to yourself when you’re anxious, and try offering the same words you’d give a good friend. Self-kindness isn’t indulgent — it’s one of the most reliable ways to calm a frightened mind.

If anxious thoughts are a regular part of your days, please know you don’t have to manage them alone. Therapy can help you understand where they come from and gently loosen their grip.
